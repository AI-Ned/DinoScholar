# Hunters of Rivers and Coasts

Not all predatory dinosaurs hunted on dry land. The **Spinosauridae** evolved a suite of adaptations for pursuing prey in rivers, deltas, and coastal environments — making them the most aquatic of all known non-avian dinosaurs.

## Spinosauridae: Fish-Eating Giants

The spinosaur family includes two main lineages:

- **Spinosaurinae**: *Spinosaurus*, *Sigilmassasaurus* — predominantly African and South American forms with elongated, crocodile-like snouts.
- **Baryonychinae**: *Baryonyx*, *Suchomimus* — more widespread forms retaining some features intermediate between typical theropods and the highly specialised spinosaurines.

## Aquatic Adaptations of *Spinosaurus*

Recent discoveries have radically reshaped our understanding of *Spinosaurus*. Once depicted as a typical bipedal theropod with a sail, it is now recognised as a **semi-aquatic pursuit predator**:

- **Paddle-like tail**: The tail bore tall neural spines and chevrons forming a broad, flexible fin — ideal for propulsion through water, comparable to the tail of a newt or crocodilian.
- **Dense bones**: Unlike most theropods, *Spinosaurus* had **osteosclerotic** (abnormally dense) long bones — reducing buoyancy, much as manatees and hippos use dense ribs to stay submerged.
- **Retracted nostrils**: Positioned far back on the skull, allowing the animal to breathe while mostly submerged.
- **Pressure-sensing snout**: Pits on the tip of the snout resemble those of crocodilians, which detect water-pressure changes from nearby fish movements.
- **Conical, interlocking teeth**: Designed for gripping slippery fish rather than tearing flesh from terrestrial prey.
- **Short hind limbs**: Proportionally shorter than any other large theropod, poorly suited for sustained terrestrial locomotion.

### The Sail

The sail (or hump) of *Spinosaurus* — formed by neural spines up to 1.8 metres tall — remains debated:

- **Display**: The most widely accepted hypothesis. Visible above the waterline, the sail would signal fitness to rivals and mates.
- **Thermoregulation**: The vascularised bone could have dissipated or absorbed heat, but surface area calculations suggest this was secondary at best.
- **Fat storage**: Some researchers suggest the spines supported a fleshy hump (like a bison) rather than a thin sail.

## Baryonyx: The Claw that Changed Everything

*Baryonyx walkeri* was discovered in 1983 in a Surrey clay pit. Its massive thumb claw (30 cm along the outer curve) gave it its name — "heavy claw." The specimen also preserved:

- **Acid-etched fish scales** in the gut region, directly confirming a piscivorous diet.
- **Partially digested bones of a juvenile *Iguanodon***, showing that spinosaurs were **opportunistic feeders**, not exclusively fish-eaters.

This mix of fish and dinosaur remains paints a picture of a versatile predator — analogous to modern grizzly bears that fish for salmon but also hunt terrestrial prey.

## Ecological Niche: Why Go Aquatic?

The Cretaceous river systems of North Africa supported an extraordinary biomass of **large fish**:

- *Mawsonia* — a coelacanth up to 4 metres long.
- *Onchopristis* — a sawfish reaching 8 metres.
- Giant lungfish and other large freshwater species.

These rivers flowed through lush delta environments comparable to modern mangrove systems. For a large predator, exploiting this aquatic bounty reduced competition with terrestrial apex predators like *Carcharodontosaurus*, which shared the same ecosystems.

This **niche partitioning** — multiple large predators coexisting by exploiting different prey resources — is a key ecological concept. The Kem Kem beds of Morocco preserve at least three large theropods (*Spinosaurus*, *Carcharodontosaurus*, *Deltadromeus*) alongside giant crocodilians and pterosaurs — one of the most predator-rich ecosystems known.

## Other Semi-Aquatic Strategies

Beyond spinosaurs, other dinosaurs showed aquatic tendencies:

- **Halszkaraptor escuilliei**: A small dromaeosaurid from Mongolia with flipper-like forelimbs and a swan-like neck, interpreted as a semi-aquatic fish-eater.
- **Liaoningosaurus paradoxus**: A tiny ankylosaur from China found with fish remains in its gut — suggesting at least occasional aquatic foraging.

## Key Takeaways

- Spinosaurids were the only large theropods to evolve extensive aquatic adaptations.
- *Spinosaurus* is now understood as a tail-propelled, semi-aquatic predator — a radical departure from traditional bipedal theropod body plans.
- Piscivory allowed spinosaurs to coexist with terrestrial apex predators through niche partitioning.
- Spinosaurs were opportunistic — fish were the primary diet, but terrestrial prey was also taken.

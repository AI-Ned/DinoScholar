# Southern Killers: Abelisaurids & Carcharodontosaurids

While tyrannosaurs dominated the northern continents in the Late Cretaceous, the southern landmasses — fragments of the ancient supercontinent **Gondwana** — hosted their own lineages of fearsome apex predators.

## Carcharodontosaurids: The Shark-Toothed Giants

The family **Carcharodontosauridae** (literally "shark-toothed lizards") included some of the largest terrestrial carnivores ever to live. They were allosauroid theropods that reached their peak diversity in the middle Cretaceous.

### Key Species

| Species | Age (Mya) | Length (m) | Region |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Carcharodontosaurus saharicus* | 100–94 | 12.0 | North Africa |
| *Giganotosaurus carolinii* | 97 | 13.0 | Argentina |
| *Mapusaurus roseae* | 97 | 12.5 | Argentina |
| *Acrocanthosaurus atokensis* | 116–110 | 11.5 | North America |
| *Meraxes gigas* | 93 | 11.0 | Argentina |

### Hunting Strategy

Carcharodontosaurids had a fundamentally different killing approach from tyrannosaurs:

- **Blade-like teeth** with serrated edges, designed for slicing flesh rather than crushing bone. They made wide, debilitating wounds.
- **Relatively weaker bite** than tyrannosaurs — compensated by powerful neck musculature that allowed them to use the head like a hatchet, delivering slashing bites.
- **Long, three-fingered forelimbs** — unlike the vestigial arms of tyrannosaurs, carcharodontosaurid arms were functional grappling tools.

The *Mapusaurus* bonebed in Argentina preserves at least seven individuals of varying age, which some researchers interpret as a **social group** capable of tackling the giant sauropod *Argentinosaurus*.

### Decline

Carcharodontosaurids disappeared from most landmasses by roughly 90 million years ago. The reasons remain unclear — climate shifts, competition, and faunal turnover are all proposed. Their extinction in North America opened the ecological door for tyrannosaurs to rise to dominance.

## Abelisaurids: The Horned Predators

The **Abelisauridae** were the quintessential Gondwanan predators of the Late Cretaceous. These ceratosaurs evolved a bizarre suite of features that made them strikingly different from any Northern Hemisphere theropod.

### Distinctive Anatomy

- **Skull ornamentation**: Many abelisaurids bore horns, knobs, or rugose bone textures on their skulls — likely for display and intraspecific combat.
- **Vestigial forelimbs**: Even more reduced than in tyrannosaurs. *Carnotaurus* had forearms shorter than its hands, effectively useless for prey capture.
- **Deep, bulldog-like skulls**: Short and tall rather than long and narrow, with powerful jaw adductor muscles.
- **Speed-built legs**: Long tibiae and metatarsals suggest abelisaurids were fast pursuit predators.

### Key Species

| Species | Age (Mya) | Length (m) | Notable Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Carnotaurus sastrei* | 72–69 | 8.0 | Bull-like horns above the eyes |
| *Majungasaurus crenatissimus* | 70–66 | 7.0 | Cannibalism evidence |
| *Abelisaurus comahuensis* | 83–80 | 7.5 | Namesake of the family |
| *Skorpiovenator bustingorryi* | 95 | 7.5 | Nearly complete skeleton |
| *Rajasaurus narmadensis* | 70–66 | 7.0 | One of the last Indian dinosaurs |

### Cannibalism in *Majungasaurus*

Tooth-marked bones of *Majungasaurus* bearing the distinctive tooth pattern of other *Majungasaurus* individuals provide unambiguous evidence of **cannibalism** in this species. Whether this reflects active predation on conspecifics or scavenging of dead individuals is debated, but the frequency of marked bones suggests it was not a rare occurrence.

### Skin and Appearance

A remarkable *Carnotaurus* specimen preserves extensive skin impressions. The skin was covered in rows of **non-overlapping, pebbly scales** of varying sizes — some regions bore larger feature scales arranged in rows. No evidence of feathers was found, supporting the idea that large abelisaurids had scaly integument.

## Ecological Roles Compared

| Feature | Tyrannosaurs | Carcharodontosaurids | Abelisaurids |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bite style** | Bone-crushing | Flesh-slicing | Powerful snap-bite |
| **Forelimbs** | Tiny but muscular | Long and functional | Vestigial |
| **Vision** | Binocular | Lateral | Lateral |
| **Top speed estimate** | 20–28 km/h | Unknown | 30–48 km/h |
| **Distribution** | Laurasia | Gondwana + Laurasia | Gondwana |
| **Peak era** | Late Cretaceous | Mid-Cretaceous | Late Cretaceous |

## Key Takeaways

- Carcharodontosaurids were colossal flesh-slicers that may have hunted cooperatively to tackle giant sauropods.
- Abelisaurids were fast, horned, short-armed predators unique to Gondwana.
- Different continents produced radically different solutions to the apex-predator problem.
- The decline of carcharodontosaurids directly enabled the tyrannosaur takeover in the north.

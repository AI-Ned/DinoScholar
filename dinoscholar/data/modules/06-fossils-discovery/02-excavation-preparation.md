# Excavation and Preparation

Extracting a fossil from its rocky matrix and preparing it for study is one of the most skilled and time-consuming activities in paleontology. A single large dinosaur skeleton may require years of preparation work.

## Field Excavation

### Exposing the Specimen

Once a significant find is confirmed, excavation begins:

1. **Overburden removal**: Rock above the fossil layer is carefully removed using picks, shovels, and sometimes even heavy machinery for thick overburden.
2. **Close work**: As the fossil layer is approached, tools scale down — rock hammers, chisels, awls, brushes, and dental picks.
3. **Consolidation**: Exposed bone is immediately stabilised with **Vinac** (polyvinyl butyral dissolved in acetone) or **Paraloid B-72** (an acrylic resin). These penetrate porous bone and harden it against breakage.

### Jacketing

The most important field preservation technique is **plaster jacketing**:

1. The fossil and a surrounding margin of matrix are isolated as blocks, leaving them supported on pedestals of unexcavated rock.
2. The exposed surfaces are covered with **separator** — wet tissue paper or aluminium foil — to prevent plaster from bonding to the bone.
3. Strips of **burlap (hessian) soaked in plaster of Paris** are layered over the block, forming a rigid protective shell.
4. The block is undercut, flipped, and the underside is also jacketed.
5. Each jacket is **labelled** with orientation marks, field number, and a schematic drawing of contents.

Large jackets may weigh hundreds of kilograms and require specialised equipment — hand-built cradles, winches, or even helicopter lift — for transport.

### Field Documentation

Throughout excavation, detailed records are maintained:

- **Quarry maps**: Scale drawings showing the position of every bone and significant fragment.
- **Photography**: Each stage documented from multiple angles.
- **Field notes**: Observations on bone condition, matrix type, associated fauna/flora, and taphonomy.
- **3D photogrammetry**: Increasingly, sites are recorded as 3D digital models.

## Laboratory Preparation

Back in the museum or university lab, the real precision work begins.

### Mechanical Preparation

The most traditional approach uses hand tools under **binocular microscopes**:

- **Pneumatic air scribes**: Miniature jackhammers powered by compressed air, vibrating a hardened steel tip against the rock matrix. Different tips for different conditions.
- **Carbide needles**: For fine detail work around delicate features.
- **Brushes**: Soft brushes clear debris without scratching bone surfaces.

A single skull may require **1,000–3,000 hours** of mechanical preparation.

### Chemical Preparation

For bones embedded in calcareous (limestone) matrix:

- **Acetic acid** (dilute, 5–10%) dissolves calcium carbonate without affecting bone phosphate. The specimen is cycled through acid baths and rinses, with exposed bone surfaces protected by consolidant between sessions.
- **Formic acid** is sometimes used for more resistant matrices.
- Chemical preparation can reveal exquisite detail — individual sutures, foramina (nerve/blood vessel openings), and surface textures invisible before treatment.

### CT Scanning

Non-invasive **computed tomography (CT)** scanning has revolutionised preparation:

- Synchrotron and micro-CT scanners can image internal structures — brain endocasts, inner ear anatomy, pneumatic cavities — without removing any rock.
- Digital segmentation software allows virtual "preparation" — separating bone from matrix computationally.
- This is especially valuable for rare or fragile specimens where physical preparation would risk damage.

## Moulding and Casting

### Why Cast?

Most mounted skeletons in museums are **casts** (replicas), not original fossils. Reasons include:

- Originals are too heavy for display mounts (fossilised bone is denser than fresh bone due to mineral replacement).
- Originals are too fragile or scientifically valuable to risk on permanent display.
- Casts allow multiple institutions to display the same specimen.

### The Process

1. **Moulding**: Flexible silicone rubber moulds are made of each bone, capturing surface detail down to sub-millimetre resolution.
2. **Casting**: The moulds are filled with resin (polyester, epoxy, or polyurethane), sometimes mixed with fillers for weight reduction.
3. **Painting**: Casts are painted to match the original fossil's colour and texture.

Modern **3D printing** increasingly supplements traditional moulding — digital scan data can produce replicas directly, and missing elements can be digitally reconstructed.

## Key Takeaways

- Field excavation uses plaster jackets to protect fossils during transport — a technique virtually unchanged since the 1877 Bone Wars.
- Laboratory preparation is extraordinarily time-intensive, often measured in thousands of hours per specimen.
- CT scanning allows non-invasive study of internal anatomy, sometimes eliminating the need for physical preparation.
- Most museum-displayed skeletons are high-quality casts, preserving originals for research.

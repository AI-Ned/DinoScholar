# Feeding and Digestion

The diversity of dinosaur diets is reflected in an extraordinary range of skull shapes, tooth designs, and digestive strategies. A dinosaur's teeth tell us nearly as much about its lifestyle as any other element of its anatomy.

## Carnivore Adaptations

### Tyrannosaurid Strategy: Crush and Swallow
- T. rex had **banana-shaped, bone-crushing teeth** with serrated edges
- Its bite force exceeded 12,800 pounds — enough to shatter bone
- Coprolites (fossilized dung) containing crushed bone confirm it consumed entire carcasses
- Wide skull with forward-facing eyes provided binocular vision for depth perception

### Dromaeosaurid Strategy: Grapple and Slice
- Recurved, blade-like teeth for slicing flesh
- The enlarged sickle claw on each foot pinned and restrained prey
- Likely used the claw to maintain grip rather than disembowel (as once thought)

### Spinosaurid Strategy: Snatch and Gulp
- Conical, unserrated teeth — designed for gripping slippery fish
- Long, narrow snouts with pressure-sensitive pits (like crocodilians)
- Some, like Baryonyx, also ate land animals based on gut contents

## Herbivore Adaptations

### Sauropod Strategy: Strip and Swallow
- **Peg-like or pencil-like teeth** for stripping foliage (Diplodocus)
- **Spoon-shaped teeth** for coarser vegetation (Brachiosaurus)
- Food was swallowed without chewing — no complex jaw motion
- **Gastroliths** (stomach stones) may have aided digestion in some species
- Enormous gut capacity for fermenting plant material

### Hadrosaur Strategy: Chew and Grind
- **Dental batteries** with hundreds of tightly-packed teeth
- Multiple teeth stacked in columns, with worn teeth continuously replaced
- Complex jaw motion allowed grinding similar to a mammal's chewing
- The most sophisticated feeding apparatus of any dinosaur group

### Ceratopsian Strategy: Shear and Slice
- A sharp, parrot-like beak for cropping tough vegetation
- Dental batteries behind the beak for processing food
- Powerful jaw muscles anchored to the bony frill
- Could process extremely tough plant material including woody plants

## Omnivore Adaptations

Some dinosaurs ate both plants and animals:
- **Ornithomimids** — Toothless beaks suggesting varied diet; possible filter feeding
- **Oviraptorids** — Strong beaks that could crack eggs, nuts, or shellfish
- **Pachycephalosaurs** — Small teeth suggesting a mixed diet

## Digestive Strategies

Since soft tissues rarely fossilize, digestive systems are largely inferred:
- **Theropods** likely had relatively short, simple digestive tracts (similar to modern birds of prey)
- **Sauropods** needed enormous gut volumes to ferment low-quality food — their barrel-shaped bodies reflect this
- **Hadrosaurs** had complex oral processing, reducing the need for prolonged gut fermentation

> **Key Takeaway:** Dinosaur feeding adaptations evolved to exploit every available food source. From the bone-crushing bite of T. rex to the precision grinding of hadrosaur dental batteries, tooth and skull design directly reflected dietary strategy.

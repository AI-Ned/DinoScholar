# Skin, Scales, and Feathers

One of the most dramatic shifts in dinosaur science has been the realization that many dinosaurs — far more than previously imagined — were feathered. At the same time, spectacular skin impressions reveal diverse scale patterns in other groups.

## Scales and Skin

Many dinosaurs had traditional reptilian skin:

### Hadrosaur Skin
- Multiple "mummy" specimens preserve extensive skin impressions
- Shows pebbly, non-overlapping scales of varying sizes
- The Edmontosaurus "Dakota" mummy preserves skin over much of its body
- One specimen reveals a fleshy comb or wattle on its head — a structure invisible in the skeleton

### Ceratopsian Skin
- Limited skin impressions suggest small, rounded scales
- The scale pattern varied across different body regions

### Ankylosaur Armour
- The most extreme skin modification in dinosaurs
- **Osteoderms** — Bony plates embedded in the dermis, covered by keratinous sheaths
- Ranged from small nodules to large plates and spines
- In Ankylosaurus, even the eyelids were armoured

### Sauropod Skin
- Known from several titanosaur specimens
- Shows non-overlapping, hexagonal scales of varying sizes
- Some titanosaurs had small osteoderms scattered across their bodies

## The Feathered Revolution

### Evidence from China
The Yixian and Jiufotang Formations of Liaoning Province, China, have produced dozens of feathered dinosaur specimens preserved in extraordinary detail:

- **Sinosauropteryx** (1996) — The first feathered dinosaur described. Simple filamentous (hair-like) feathers covered its body.
- **Microraptor** — Complex, asymmetric flight feathers on all four limbs
- **Sinornithosaurus** — Preserved with multiple feather types
- **Yutyrannus** — A 9-metre tyrannosauroid covered in filamentous feathers — proving that even very large dinosaurs could be feathered

### Types of Feathers

Dinosaur feathers existed in a range of complexity:

1. **Stage 1 — Simple filaments** — Hair-like structures for insulation (Sinosauropteryx)
2. **Stage 2 — Tufted filaments** — Down-like clusters (some ornithischians)
3. **Stage 3 — Branched feathers** — With a central shaft and barbs
4. **Stage 4 — Pennaceous feathers** — Flat, vane-like feathers (Microraptor, Archaeopteryx)
5. **Stage 5 — Asymmetric flight feathers** — The key adaptation for powered flight

### Which Dinosaurs Had Feathers?

- **Almost certainly feathered**: All maniraptorans (dromaeosaurids, oviraptorids, troodontids, birds)
- **Likely feathered**: Most coelurosaurs (including early tyrannosaurs)
- **Possibly feathered**: Some ornithischians (filamentous structures found in *Tianyulong* and *Kulindadromeus*)
- **Probably scaled**: Adult large tyrannosaurs (skin impressions from T. rex show scales), most sauropods, ankylosaurs

## Dinosaur Colours

Fossilized melanosomes allow scientists to reconstruct actual dinosaur colours:

- **Microraptor** — Iridescent black (like a crow)
- **Sinosauropteryx** — Reddish-brown back with a pale belly and a banded tail
- **Anchiornis** — Complex pattern of black, grey, white, and rufous (reddish) patches

This is one of the most exciting frontiers in paleontology: recovering the actual appearance of animals extinct for millions of years.

> **Key Takeaway:** Dinosaur integument ranged from traditional reptilian scales to complex, colourful feathers. The discovery of widespread feathering in theropods has fundamentally changed our image of dinosaurs, while melanosome analysis is revealing their actual colours for the first time.

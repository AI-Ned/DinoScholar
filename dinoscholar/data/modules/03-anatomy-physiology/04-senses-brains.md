# Senses and Brains

Until recently, dinosaurs were stereotyped as dim-witted creatures. Modern technology — particularly CT scanning — has revealed that many dinosaurs had sophisticated brains and keen senses.

## Brain Size and Intelligence

Dinosaur brain size is estimated from **endocasts** — casts of the interior of the skull cavity. Key findings:

- **Encephalization quotient (EQ)** measures brain size relative to body size
- Most dinosaurs had EQs comparable to modern reptiles
- **Troodontids and dromaeosaurids** had EQs approaching those of modern birds — suggesting considerable intelligence
- **Sauropods** had relatively tiny brains for their body size (Stegosaurus' brain was roughly walnut-sized)
- **T. rex** had a large brain for a non-avian dinosaur, with well-developed olfactory and visual centres

The old myth of Stegosaurus having a "second brain" in its hip is exactly that — a myth. The enlarged cavity in its hip (glycogen body) is found in all birds and served a different function.

## Vision

Dinosaur visual capabilities varied enormously:

- **Binocular vision** — Forward-facing eyes (T. rex, dromaeosaurids) provided depth perception for hunting. T. rex had binocular overlap comparable to a modern hawk.
- **Monocular vision** — Side-facing eyes (many herbivores) provided a wide field of view for predator detection
- **Colour vision** — Birds have four types of colour receptors (vs. three in humans). This tetrachromatic vision likely originated in their dinosaur ancestors.
- **Night vision** — The large eye sockets of some species (T. rex, troodontids) suggest good low-light vision via large eyes

## Hearing

Dinosaur hearing is inferred from inner ear anatomy:

- **Hadrosaur crests** — The hollow crests of Parasaurolophus and Corythosaurus were resonating chambers that produced and detected low-frequency sounds (~30 Hz). CT scans reveal complex internal airways tuned to specific frequencies.
- **Inner ear structure** — The length of the cochlea (hearing organ) in different species indicates frequency sensitivity. Some theropods could hear a wide range of frequencies.
- **Infrasound** — Large dinosaurs may have communicated using very low-frequency sounds that could travel long distances through forests and over plains.

## Smell

- **T. rex** had enormous olfactory bulbs — the parts of the brain responsible for processing smell. It may have had one of the keenest senses of smell of any animal.
- This has implications for the old scavenger vs. predator debate: both extreme scavengers (vultures) and active predators (wolves) rely heavily on smell.

## Touch and Other Senses

- **Spinosaurid snouts** contained pressure-sensitive pits similar to those in crocodilian snouts, allowing them to detect vibrations in water — useful for catching fish
- **Ceratopsian frills** were richly vascularized, possibly serving as display organs and/or thermoregulatory surfaces

> **Key Takeaway:** Many dinosaurs, particularly theropods, possessed highly developed senses including binocular vision, acute hearing, and sharp smell. Brain-to-body ratios in maniraptorans approached those of modern birds, suggesting intelligence levels far beyond the old 'dim-witted reptile' stereotype.

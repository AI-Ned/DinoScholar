# Triassic: Dawn of the Dinosaurs

The Triassic Period (252–201 Mya) began in the wake of devastation. The Permian–Triassic extinction — the "Great Dying" — had killed over 90% of marine species and 70% of terrestrial vertebrate species. Into this emptied world, the ancestors of dinosaurs would emerge and, by the end of the period, take their first tentative steps toward global dominance.

## A Post-Apocalyptic World

The early Triassic landscape was harsh and desolate. Recovery from the Great Dying took 10–15 million years. The dominant land animals were not dinosaurs but archosaurs and therapsids (mammal-like reptiles):

- **Rauisuchians** — Large, crocodile-line predators that were the top terrestrial carnivores
- **Aetosaurs** — Armoured, herbivorous archosaurs
- **Cynodonts** — Advanced therapsids ancestral to mammals
- **Rhynchosaurs** — Beaked herbivorous reptiles

## Enter the Dinosaurs

The first true dinosaurs appeared in the Middle to Late Triassic, around 230 Mya. They were relatively small and inconspicuous:

- **Eoraptor** (~230 Mya, Argentina) — A roughly 1-metre-long omnivore, one of the earliest known dinosaurs
- **Herrerasaurus** (~230 Mya, Argentina) — A 3-metre predator, among the first large dinosauriform carnivores
- **Coelophysis** (~215 Mya, North America) — A slender, agile predator known from hundreds of specimens at Ghost Ranch

These early dinosaurs had key advantages:
1. **Upright posture** — Legs directly beneath the body rather than sprawling to the side, providing efficient locomotion
2. **Bipedality** — Many early dinosaurs walked on two legs, freeing their forelimbs
3. **Possible endothermy** — Growing evidence suggests elevated metabolic rates

## Late Triassic Diversification

By the late Triassic, dinosaurs had diversified into the major lineages:
- Early **theropods** — small to medium bipedal predators
- Early **sauropodomorphs** — such as *Plateosaurus*, showing the beginnings of the trend toward giant herbivory
- Early **ornithischians** — small, lightly-built herbivores

However, dinosaurs were still far from dominant. They shared their world with diverse competitors and were often outnumbered by other archosaurs.

## The End-Triassic Extinction

At 201 Mya, another mass extinction struck — possibly caused by massive volcanic eruptions associated with the breakup of Pangaea (the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province). This event eliminated many of the dinosaurs' competitors, including:
- Most rauisuchians
- Most large amphibians
- Many marine reptile groups

The dinosaurs survived. With their competitors removed, they radiated explosively in the Early Jurassic, beginning their 135-million-year dominance.

> **Key Takeaway:** Dinosaurs evolved during the Triassic but were initially minor players in ecosystems dominated by other groups. The end-Triassic extinction cleared the playing field, allowing dinosaurs to diversify and dominate from the Jurassic onward.

# Mesozoic Climate and Ecosystems

The Mesozoic climate was fundamentally different from today's. It was a **greenhouse world** — warmer, with higher CO₂ levels, no permanent polar ice, and higher sea levels. Understanding this climate context is essential for understanding how dinosaurs lived.

## A Warmer World

Average global temperatures during the Mesozoic were significantly higher than today:
- **Triassic**: 3–6°C warmer than present; extreme aridity in continental interiors
- **Jurassic**: 5–8°C warmer; more humid, lush forests spread
- **Cretaceous**: 6–10°C warmer; tropical conditions extended to 60° latitude

There were **no polar ice caps** for most of the Mesozoic. Forests grew within the Arctic and Antarctic circles, though these regions experienced months of darkness in winter.

## CO₂ and the Greenhouse Effect

Atmospheric CO₂ during the Mesozoic was 2–8 times higher than today's levels. This was driven by:
- Intense volcanic activity at mid-ocean ridges (continental breakup)
- Large igneous province eruptions
- The absence of major carbon-sequestering ice sheets

The elevated CO₂ created a powerful greenhouse effect, keeping temperatures high globally.

## Mesozoic Flora

The plant world underwent a dramatic transformation during the Mesozoic:

### Triassic and Jurassic Vegetation
- **Conifers** — Dominated many forests
- **Ferns** — Covered forest floors and open areas
- **Cycads** — Palm-like plants common in warm climates
- **Ginkgoes** — Widespread (now reduced to a single surviving species)

### Cretaceous Revolution
- **Flowering plants (angiosperms)** appeared ~130 Mya and diversified explosively
- By the Late Cretaceous, angiosperms dominated many ecosystems
- Co-evolution with pollinating insects created complex ecological networks
- New food sources drove the diversification of herbivorous dinosaurs

## Ecosystem Structure

Mesozoic ecosystems were structured differently from modern ones:

**The food web:**
- **Primary producers** — Conifers, ferns, cycads, and (later) flowering plants
- **Primary consumers** — Sauropods, hadrosaurs, ceratopsians, ankylosaurs, stegosaurs
- **Secondary consumers** — Small to medium theropods
- **Apex predators** — Large theropods (Allosaurus, T. rex, Giganotosaurus)

**Key differences from modern ecosystems:**
- Herbivores reached much larger body sizes (tens of tonnes)
- The absence of grass until very late in the Cretaceous meant no grassland ecosystems
- Insect diversity was high but different from today (no social bees until the Cretaceous)

## Dinosaurs in Polar Regions

Perhaps surprisingly, dinosaurs lived in polar regions — though these areas were much warmer than today:
- Fossils found in Alaska, Antarctica, and Arctic Australia
- Polar dinosaurs experienced months of darkness in winter
- Some species may have migrated seasonally; others likely remained year-round
- Smaller polar dinosaurs may have had enhanced insulation (feathers, fat)

> **Key Takeaway:** The Mesozoic was a greenhouse world with warm temperatures, high CO₂, and no polar ice. The rise of flowering plants in the Cretaceous transformed terrestrial ecosystems and drove the diversification of the dinosaurs that fed on them.

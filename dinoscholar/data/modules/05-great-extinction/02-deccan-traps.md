# Volcanism: The Deccan Traps

The Chicxulub impact was not the only catastrophic event near the end of the Cretaceous. In what is now west-central India, one of the largest volcanic eruptions in Earth's history was already underway — the **Deccan Traps**.

## What Are the Deccan Traps?

The Deccan Traps are a **Large Igneous Province (LIP)** — a massive accumulation of basaltic lava flows covering roughly 500,000 km² of modern India (originally perhaps 1.5 million km²). The lava pile reaches thicknesses of over 2,000 metres in places.

The name "Traps" comes from the Swedish word *trappa* (staircase), describing the step-like landscape formed by successive lava flows.

## Timing and Duration

Precise radiometric dating (using argon-argon and uranium-lead methods) has established that:

- Eruptions began roughly **67 Mya** — about 1 million years before the K–Pg boundary.
- The most intense eruptive phase occurred between **66.3 and 65.5 Mya**, straddling the extinction boundary.
- Total eruption duration was approximately **1–2 million years**, with most of the volume concentrated in relatively brief pulses.

Crucially, **about 70–80% of the total lava volume** erupted in the ~500,000 years after the Chicxulub impact — raising the question of whether the impact itself *triggered* the intensified volcanism.

## Environmental Effects

Flood basalt eruptions release enormous quantities of **volcanic gases**:

| Gas | Effect |
|---|---|
| CO₂ | Long-term greenhouse warming (thousands of years) |
| SO₂ | Short-term aerosol cooling and acid rain (years to decades per pulse) |
| HCl / HF | Ozone depletion, acid rain |
| Mercury | Neurotoxin; mercury spikes found at the K–Pg boundary |

Each eruptive pulse would have caused a cycle of:
1. **Cooling** (SO₂ aerosols reflecting sunlight) lasting years
2. Followed by **warming** (CO₂ accumulation) lasting millennia

This repeated thermal oscillation — sometimes called **volcanic winters followed by greenhouse summers** — would have stressed ecosystems with rapid climate instability.

## The Debate: Impact vs Volcanism

The scientific community is broadly divided into three camps:

### Camp 1: Impact Was the Primary Cause
- The extinction is geologically instantaneous — within a few thousand years of the boundary, consistent with impact-timescale disruption.
- Species show no gradual decline toward the boundary in well-sampled fossil records.
- The Deccan eruptions were already underway for ~800,000 years before the extinction — if volcanism alone were sufficient, the extinction should have started earlier.

### Camp 2: Volcanism As a Contributing Factor (Press-Pulse Model)
- The Deccan eruptions created long-term environmental **stress** (the "press") that weakened ecosystems.
- The Chicxulub impact delivered the fatal **pulse** to already-stressed communities.
- This model explains why the end-Cretaceous extinction was more severe than other large impacts (there were other major impacts in Earth's history that did not cause mass extinctions).

### Camp 3: Volcanism As the Primary Cause
- A minority position, but some researchers argue that Deccan volcanism alone could account for the extinction.
- They point to mercury anomalies and climate proxies showing ecosystem stress before the impact.
- The other four major Phanerozoic mass extinctions (End-Ordovician, Late Devonian, End-Permian, End-Triassic) are all associated with LIP volcanism — not impacts.

## Did the Impact Trigger Eruptions?

A provocative hypothesis suggests that the seismic energy from Chicxulub (equivalent to a magnitude ~11 earthquake) could have **destabilised the Deccan magma plumbing system**, amplifying eruptions. Evidence:

- The most voluminous Deccan lava flows post-date the impact.
- Similar impact-triggered volcanism has been proposed for other impact events.
- However, modelling suggests the energy transfer is marginal — the hypothesis remains unproven.

## Other Large Igneous Provinces and Extinctions

The Deccan-impact debate gains perspective when placed in context:

| Mass Extinction | Age (Mya) | Associated LIP | Associated Impact? |
|---|---|---|---|
| End-Permian | 252 | Siberian Traps | None confirmed |
| End-Triassic | 201 | Central Atlantic Magmatic Province | None confirmed |
| End-Cretaceous | 66 | Deccan Traps | Chicxulub (confirmed) |

The end-Cretaceous is the **only** major extinction with strong evidence for both a LIP and a major impact — which both complicates and enriches the scientific debate.

## Key Takeaways

- The Deccan Traps represent one of the largest volcanic events in Earth's history, erupting across the K–Pg boundary.
- Volcanic gases caused repeated cycles of cooling and warming, stressing late-Cretaceous ecosystems.
- The dominant scientific view is that volcanism weakened ecosystems (press) while the impact delivered the killing blow (pulse).
- The relationship between Chicxulub and Deccan eruption intensity remains an active research question.

# Reading the K–Pg Boundary

The Cretaceous–Paleogene (K–Pg) boundary is one of the most intensively studied horizons in the geological record. At sites around the world, a thin clay layer — often just a few centimetres thick — records the greatest catastrophe in the last 66 million years. Learning to read this layer is an exercise in geological forensics.

## Where to Find It

The K–Pg boundary has been identified at over **350 sites** worldwide — in marine sediments, terrestrial flood plains, and lacustrine deposits. Key localities include:

| Site | Country | Setting | Notable Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gubbio | Italy | Deep marine limestone | Original iridium discovery site |
| Stevns Klint | Denmark | Marine chalk | UNESCO World Heritage Site |
| El Kef | Tunisia | Marine marl | Global Boundary Stratotype (GSSP) |
| Hell Creek | Montana, USA | Terrestrial floodplain | Rich dinosaur-to-mammal fossil transition |
| Tanis | North Dakota, USA | Estuarine | Claimed same-day impact deposits |

The **Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point (GSSP)** — the official reference point defining the K–Pg boundary — is at El Kef, Tunisia, chosen for its continuous marine sedimentation and well-preserved boundary markers.

## The Physical Signature

### Iridium Anomaly

Iridium (Ir) is a **platinum-group element** that is:
- Extremely rare in Earth's crust (~0.02 parts per billion)
- Relatively abundant in chondritic meteorites (~500 ppb)

At the K–Pg boundary, iridium concentrations spike to **30–130 times background levels**, forming a sharp, unmistakable geochemical fingerprint.

### Shocked Quartz

Quartz grains at the boundary display **planar deformation features (PDFs)** — sets of parallel microscopic lamellae caused by shock pressures exceeding 10 gigapascals. These pressures are achievable only by:
- Hypervelocity impacts
- Nuclear detonations

No volcanic process generates sufficient pressure for PDFs, making shocked quartz a diagnostic impact marker.

### Spherules and Tektites

Millimetre-sized **glass spherules** (solidified droplets of melted rock) are found in boundary layers, particularly in sites closer to the impact. In Central America and the Gulf Coast, spherule layers can be **several centimetres thick** and grade into coarser ejecta.

### Tsunami Deposits

In the Gulf of Mexico and Caribbean, the boundary includes **chaotic, thick sedimentary units** — jumbled mixtures of sand, shell fragments, and reworked sediment interpreted as mega-tsunami deposits. Some sequences are tens of metres thick.

## The Chemical Signature

Beyond iridium, the boundary clay contains:

- **Chromium, cobalt, nickel** anomalies — consistent with a chondritic impactor.
- **Osmium isotope ratios** (¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os) — the boundary shows values matching meteoritic, not terrestrial, sources.
- **Amino acids** — including non-biological isomers found in carbonaceous chondrite meteorites (e.g., α-aminoisobutyric acid).
- **Mercury enrichment** — possibly linked to both impact and Deccan volcanism.

## The Biological Signature

### Below the Boundary (Latest Cretaceous)

Rich faunas and floras: diverse dinosaurs, ammonites, rudist bivalves, calcareous nannoplankton (coccolithophorids), and planktonic foraminifera.

### At the Boundary

An abrupt disappearance of:
- All non-avian dinosaur lineages
- Ammonites
- Most calcareous plankton species
- Many groups of marine invertebrates

### Above the Boundary (Earliest Paleogene)

- **Fern spore spike**: In terrestrial sections worldwide, fern spores dominate pollen assemblages immediately above the boundary — the "fern spike." Ferns are pioneer colonisers of disturbed landscapes (as seen after modern volcanic eruptions), indicating devastated terrestrial vegetation.
- **Fungal spike**: Some sections show a spike in fungal spores, consistent with massive decay of dead organic matter.
- **"Disaster fauna"**: Low-diversity communities dominated by small, generalist species — the ecological equivalent of weeds.
- **Gradual recovery**: Full ecosystem recovery took **hundreds of thousands to millions of years**, with different groups recovering at different rates.

## The Tanis Site

The Tanis locality in North Dakota (published 2019–2022) claims to preserve deposits from **the day of impact** itself:

- Impact spherules embedded in gill rakers of paddlefish, indicating the fish were alive when spherules rained down.
- Seiche (standing wave) deposits in a river system, triggered by the seismic waves from Chicxulub.
- Fossil wood and amber fragments from trees knocked down by the event.

If confirmed, Tanis represents an unprecedented snapshot of the extinction event at hourly-to-daily resolution. The site remains controversial, with debate over interpretation of some depositional features.

## How the Boundary is Dated

The K–Pg boundary is dated at **66.043 ± 0.011 Ma** using:

- **⁴⁰Ar/³⁹Ar dating** of impact-melt glass from the boundary layer and from Chicxulub drill cores.
- **U-Pb dating** of zircon crystals in volcanic ash layers bracketing the boundary.
- **Astrochronology** — orbital tuning of sedimentary cycles to Milankovitch parameters, providing independent age constraints.

This precision — ±11,000 years — is remarkable for an event 66 million years old.

## Key Takeaways

- The K–Pg boundary is globally identifiable through iridium, shocked quartz, spherules, and abrupt fossil disappearances.
- The fern spike and disaster fauna above the boundary record the ecological devastation and early recovery.
- Dating precision of ±11,000 years confirms the synchronicity of the extinction worldwide.
- The Tanis site may capture the very day of impact, though interpretation remains debated.

# The Chicxulub Impact

Sixty-six million years ago, an asteroid roughly **10–12 kilometres in diameter** struck what is now the Yucatán Peninsula of Mexico, creating the Chicxulub crater and triggering the most famous mass extinction in Earth's history.

## Discovery of the Smoking Gun

The impact hypothesis was not always accepted. The story of its discovery is a landmark in scientific detective work:

1. **1980 — The Iridium Anomaly**: Luis and Walter Alvarez, along with Frank Asaro and Helen Michel, discovered a thin clay layer at the Cretaceous–Paleogene (K–Pg) boundary enriched in **iridium** — an element rare on Earth's surface but common in asteroids. They proposed an extraterrestrial impact as the cause of the extinction.

2. **1991 — The Crater Found**: After a decade of searching, Alan Hildebrand and colleagues identified the **Chicxulub crater** buried beneath younger sediments on the Yucatán coast. Gravity anomaly maps and drill cores confirmed a ~180 km diameter impact structure of the right age.

3. **2010 — Scientific Consensus**: A panel of 41 scientists published a comprehensive review in *Science*, concluding that the Chicxulub impact was the primary driver of the end-Cretaceous mass extinction.

## The Impact Event

### The First Seconds

The asteroid entered the atmosphere at approximately **20 km/s** (72,000 km/h). At this velocity, there was no time for atmospheric braking. The asteroid reached the surface essentially intact, striking shallow seas over a carbonate and evaporite platform.

The energy released was roughly **10 billion times** that of the Hiroshima bomb — approximately 4.2 × 10²³ joules.

### The First Minutes

- A **fireball** expanded outward at hypersonic speed, incinerating everything within 1,500 km.
- The impact excavated a **transient crater** roughly 100 km wide and 30 km deep, before gravitational collapse formed the final 180 km structure.
- A **mega-tsunami** radiated outward from the Gulf of Mexico; tsunami deposits up to 100 metres thick have been found in Texas, Alabama, and across the Caribbean.
- **Ejecta** — molten rock and vaporised target material — was launched on ballistic trajectories, re-entering the atmosphere worldwide as **spherules** (tiny glass beads) that heated the upper atmosphere to oven-like temperatures.

### The First Hours to Days

- The raining spherules triggered **global wildfires**. Soot and charcoal layers at the K–Pg boundary confirm widespread burning.
- **Sulfur aerosols** from the vaporised evaporite (gypsum) bedrock were injected into the stratosphere, reflecting sunlight and causing rapid cooling.
- Fine **dust and soot** blocked sunlight for months — an "impact winter."

### The Months to Years

- Photosynthesis collapsed on land and in the oceans, devastating food chains from the bottom up.
- Surface temperatures may have dropped by **10–15°C** during the impact winter.
- When the dust cleared, the excess CO₂ from vaporised carbonates and wildfires caused a **greenhouse rebound**, with temperatures potentially rising above pre-impact norms for thousands of years.
- **Acid rain** from sulfur and nitrogen compounds in the atmosphere acidified surface waters, devastating marine shell-builders.

## The K–Pg Boundary Layer

The physical evidence is preserved worldwide as a thin but distinctive sedimentary layer:

| Evidence | Significance |
|---|---|
| Iridium enrichment (30–130× background) | Extraterrestrial source |
| Shocked quartz grains | High-pressure impact deformation |
| Glass spherules (tektites) | Melted ejecta |
| Nickel-rich spinels | Meteoritic material |
| Soot and charcoal layer | Global wildfire evidence |
| Fern spore spike (above boundary) | Pioneer ferns colonising devastated landscapes |

## What Died and What Survived

The extinction was severe but selective:

**Victims (roughly 76% of all species)**:
- All non-avian dinosaurs
- Pterosaurs
- Marine reptiles (mosasaurs, plesiosaurs)
- Ammonites
- Many marine plankton groups (particularly calcareous forms)

**Survivors**:
- Birds (the only surviving dinosaur lineage)
- Mammals (mostly small, burrowing, or semi-aquatic forms)
- Crocodilians
- Turtles
- Amphibians
- Insects (largely unaffected)
- Freshwater ecosystems (detritus-based food chains were resilient)

The pattern suggests that survival favoured animals that were **small, could shelter underground or underwater, and did not depend on continuous photosynthesis-based food chains**.

## Key Takeaways

- The Chicxulub impact released energy equivalent to billions of nuclear weapons, triggering global firestorms, tsunamis, and an impact winter.
- The iridium anomaly, shocked quartz, and the crater itself constitute overwhelming physical evidence.
- The extinction mechanism was not the impact itself but the cascading environmental collapse — darkness, cold, acid rain, then greenhouse warming.
- About 76% of species perished, with a pattern favouring small, sheltering, and ecologically flexible survivors.

# Hypothesis Testing with Fossils

Paleontology is a **historical science** — its subjects are long dead, its evidence is fragmentary, and controlled experiments are usually impossible. Yet paleontology is every bit as rigorous as experimental sciences. The key is a different approach to hypothesis testing.

## The Logic of Historical Science

In experimental sciences (chemistry, molecular biology), hypotheses are tested by **manipulating variables** and observing outcomes. In historical science, hypotheses are tested by making **predictions about evidence that has not yet been examined** and then checking whether the evidence matches.

This approach follows the method of **multiple working hypotheses**:

1. Observe a pattern or phenomenon requiring explanation.
2. Formulate **multiple competing hypotheses**.
3. Deduce what evidence each hypothesis **predicts** should exist.
4. Search for that evidence — in existing collections, new fieldwork, or novel analytical techniques.
5. Evaluate which hypothesis best fits the total evidence.

### Example: Dinosaur Endothermy

**Observation**: Dinosaurs were large, active animals. Were they warm-blooded (endothermic) or cold-blooded (ectothermic)?

**Hypothesis A** (Endothermic): Dinosaurs maintained high body temperatures internally.
- Predicts: Fast growth rates (high bone turnover), fibrolamellar bone (rapidly deposited bone tissue with high vascularisation), an upright posture (requires sustained energy), feathered insulation.

**Hypothesis B** (Ectothermic): Dinosaurs relied on external heat sources.
- Predicts: Lamellar-zonal bone (slow, cyclical growth), low vascularisation, possible gigantothermy (mass-based heat retention).

**Evidence found**: Most dinosaurs show fibrolamellar bone histology with growth rates comparable to modern mammals. Many theropods were feathered. Upright posture is universal among dinosaurs. However, some growth lines suggest seasonal variation.

**Current conclusion**: Most dinosaurs were probably endothermic or mesothermic (intermediate), with metabolic rates higher than modern reptiles but possibly not identical to mammals. The debate continues — which is itself how science works.

## Phylogenetic Inference

Phylogenetics provides a powerful tool for testing hypotheses about the **order of evolutionary events**:

- If feathers evolved once in the common ancestor of coelurosaurs, we predict all coelurosaurs should have them (or show evidence of secondary loss).
- If flight evolved from the trees down, we predict the earliest fliers should be arboreal — testable through foot morphology, claw curvature, and brain structure.
- If tyrannosaurs were social, we predict repeated bonebeds of multiple individuals — not just occasional associations.

The logic is **deductive**: if the hypothesis is true, specific patterns must exist in the evidence.

## Taphonomic Testing

**Taphonomy** — the study of how organisms become fossils — provides crucial hypothesis-testing tools:

### Example: Were Dinosaur Bonebeds Social Groups?

When multiple individuals of the same species are found together, the default interpretation is often "herd" or "pack." But taphonomy can test this:

- **Catastrophic assemblage**: All ages and sizes together, buried simultaneously (e.g., flash flood) → supports social grouping.
- **Attritional assemblage**: Accumulated over time, biased toward the very young and very old → does not require social behaviour (similar pattern at any modern waterhole).
- **Predator traps**: If carnivores, could represent animals drawn separately to a common trap (tar pit, bog) → not social.

Each interpretation makes different predictions about bone condition, size distribution, sedimentary context, and preservation.

## Biomechanical Testing

Computer modelling allows hypotheses about extinct animal function to be tested quantitatively:

- **Finite element analysis**: A digital skull model is subjected to simulated bite forces. Stress distribution reveals whether the skull was adapted for bone-crushing (tyrannosaurs) vs. flesh-slicing (allosauroids).
- **Locomotor modelling**: Muscle reconstructions and gait simulations can estimate maximum speed, ruling out (or supporting) claims like "T. rex could run 45 km/h."
- **Fluid dynamics**: Models of the *Spinosaurus* tail show it generates significant thrust in water — supporting the aquatic hypothesis.

## Falsifiability and Self-Correction

Science advances by **falsification** — a hypothesis that cannot, even in principle, be proven wrong is not scientific.

Paleontological examples of self-correction:

- *Brontosaurus* was synonymised with *Apatosaurus* in 1903 (same animal, different names) — then **re-validated** in 2015 when more detailed analysis established sufficient differences.
- The "raptor prey restraint" hypothesis (dromaeosaurid claws for slashing) was revised to "pinning/puncturing" based on biomechanical evidence.
- *Spinosaurus* has been revised from bipedal fish-eater → knuckle-walking quadruped → tail-propelled swimmer as new fossils appeared.

Each revision represents the system working as intended — new evidence overturns old interpretations.

## Key Takeaways

- Paleontology tests hypotheses by predicting what evidence should exist and then searching for it.
- Phylogenetic, taphonomic, histological, and biomechanical analyses provide rigorous, quantitative testing tools.
- Competing hypotheses are evaluated by their fit to total evidence, not by authority or tradition.
- Self-correction — changing conclusions when evidence demands it — is a feature, not a failure, of the scientific process.

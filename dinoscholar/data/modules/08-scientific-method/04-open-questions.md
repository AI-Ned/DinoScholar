# Open Questions in Dinosaur Science

Despite over 180 years of study, fundamental questions about dinosaurs remain genuinely unanswered. These open questions are where paleontology is heading — and where the next generation of discoveries will have the greatest impact.

## 1. What Was Their Metabolism?

Were dinosaurs warm-blooded like mammals, cold-blooded like modern reptiles, or something in between?

- **Evidence for endothermy**: Fast growth rates, upright posture, feathered insulation, polar distributions.
- **Evidence for mesothermy** (intermediate): Some dinosaurs had growth lines suggesting seasonal metabolic variation. Very large dinosaurs may have used gigantothermy (mass-based heat retention).
- **Complication**: "Dinosaurs" spanned an enormous range of body sizes and ecologies. *Microraptor* (1 kg) and *Argentinosaurus* (70,000 kg) almost certainly had different metabolic strategies.

The answer is probably not a single one — dinosaur metabolism likely varied by lineage, body size, and ecology.

## 2. How Did the Largest Sauropods Function?

Sauropods like *Argentinosaurus* and *Patagotitan* reached masses of 70+ tonnes — far exceeding any modern land animal. Fundamental biomechanical questions remain:

- **Feeding**: How did they consume enough food? A 70-tonne animal would need hundreds of kilograms of vegetation daily.
- **Circulation**: How did the heart pump blood to a brain 10+ metres above chest level? Were there secondary hearts or valves in the neck?
- **Respiration**: The avian-style air sac system helped, but was it sufficient?
- **Reproduction**: How did animals this large reproduce efficiently? Growth from hatchling (a few kg) to adult (70,000 kg) required a growth factor of ~10,000×.

## 3. Why Did Non-Avian Dinosaurs Go Extinct While Birds Survived?

The extinction killed all non-avian dinosaurs but many ecologically similar birds survived. Why?

- Small body size is part of the answer — but some non-avian dinosaurs were also small.
- Seed-eating capacity may have been critical — seeds persist through environmental catastrophe while other food sources don't.
- Was flight itself a survival advantage — enabling escape from local devastation?
- Were non-avian dinosaur populations already declining before the impact? (Debated — some studies say yes, others say no.)

## 4. How Diverse Were Dinosaurs Really?

We know of ~800 valid non-avian dinosaur genera. But the fossil record is **deeply biased**:

- Only a fraction of all environments produce fossils.
- Only a fraction of fossils are exposed and discovered.
- Tropical forests — among the most biodiverse modern environments — are almost entirely absent from the dinosaur fossil record.
- Insular (island) faunas are rarely preserved.

Statistical models suggest that the true diversity of non-avian dinosaurs may have been **2,000–4,000+ genera**, meaning we've found perhaps 20–40% of what existed. Entire lineages may remain undiscovered.

## 5. What Colours and Patterns Did They Have?

Melanosome analysis has opened a window into dinosaur colour — but only for feathered species or those with preserved skin:

- What about the vast majority of dinosaurs for which no integument is preserved?
- Did large sauropods and ceratopsians have vibrant display colouration (like modern birds and reptiles)?
- How important was visual signalling vs. camouflage?

We may never know the full palette of dinosaur colour, but expanding melanosome work and chemical proxies (carotenoids, pterins) may gradually fill gaps.

## 6. Were Any Dinosaurs Truly Intelligent?

*Troodon* and similar troodontids had the **highest brain-to-body ratio** of any non-avian dinosaur. But what does that mean functionally?

- Were they capable of problem-solving, tool use, or social learning?
- Can brain size alone predict cognitive ability? (In modern animals, the relationship is complex.)
- The famous thought experiment by Dale Russell — imagining what *Troodon*'s descendants might have evolved into without the extinction — while speculative, raises genuine questions about the upper limits of dinosaur cognition.

## 7. What Sounds Did They Make?

- Lambeosaurine hadrosaurs had **hollow crests** that likely functioned as resonating chambers, producing low-frequency calls. Digital models have been used to simulate these sounds.
- But what about the majority of dinosaurs without resonating structures? Did they vocalise at all?
- Did they hiss (like crocodilians) or produce closed-mouth vocalisations (like some birds)?
- The **syrinx** (bird vocal organ) is rarely preserved — was it present in non-avian theropods?

## 8. What Happens Next?

The rate of new dinosaur species discovery has **accelerated**, not slowed:

- ~50 new species are described per year (up from ~10/year in the 1980s).
- Previously underexplored regions (Africa, the Middle East, South-East Asia, Antarctica) are beginning to yield significant finds.
- New analytical techniques extract more information from existing specimens in museum collections.

The next decades of dinosaur science will likely be the most productive yet.

## Key Takeaways

- Fundamental questions about dinosaur metabolism, biomechanics, cognition, and diversity remain actively debated.
- The fossil record is deeply incomplete — we may know less than half of all dinosaur lineages that existed.
- New technologies and new fossil localities continue to reshape our understanding at an accelerating pace.
- Open questions are not failures of knowledge — they are invitations to discovery.

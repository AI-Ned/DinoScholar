# Classifying Dinosaurs

When Richard Owen coined the word "Dinosauria" in 1842, he defined it based on just three genera: Megalosaurus, Iguanodon, and Hylaeosaurus. Today, over 1,000 non-avian dinosaur species are known, organized into a complex family tree using modern cladistic methods.

## The Two Great Orders

Traditionally, dinosaurs are divided into two major orders based on hip structure:

### Saurischia ("Lizard-Hipped")
In saurischians, the pubic bone points forward and down (as in most reptiles). This order includes:
- **Theropoda** — Bipedal, primarily carnivorous dinosaurs (T. rex, Velociraptor, Allosaurus) and **all birds**
- **Sauropodomorpha** — Long-necked herbivores ranging from Plateosaurus to Argentinosaurus

### Ornithischia ("Bird-Hipped")
In ornithischians, the pubic bone points backward (superficially like a bird's pelvis — though birds actually evolved from the saurischian line). This order includes:
- **Thyreophora** — Armoured dinosaurs (Stegosaurus, Ankylosaurus)
- **Marginocephalia** — Frilled and dome-headed dinosaurs (Triceratops, Pachycephalosaurus)
- **Ornithopoda** — The "duck-billed" and iguanodont dinosaurs (Parasaurolophus, Edmontosaurus)

The great irony: despite being called "bird-hipped," ornithischians are NOT the ancestors of birds. Birds evolved from "lizard-hipped" saurischian theropods.

## Modern Cladistics

Modern classification uses **cladistics** — grouping organisms by shared derived characteristics (synapomorphies) rather than overall similarity. Key concepts:

- **Clade** — A group containing an ancestor and ALL of its descendants (e.g., Dinosauria includes birds)
- **Sister group** — Two clades that share an immediate common ancestor
- **Basal** — A species or group that branched off early in a clade's history
- **Derived** — A species or group with many advanced features (further from the ancestral condition)

## Major Dinosaur Family Tree

```
Dinosauria
├── Ornithischia
│   ├── Thyreophora (Stegosaurs, Ankylosaurs)
│   ├── Marginocephalia (Ceratopsians, Pachycephalosaurs)
│   └── Ornithopoda (Hadrosaurs, Iguanodonts)
└── Saurischia
    ├── Sauropodomorpha (Prosauropods → Sauropods)
    └── Theropoda
        ├── Ceratosauria (Abelisaurids)
        ├── Megalosauroidea (Spinosaurids)
        └── Avetheropoda
            ├── Allosauroidea (Carcharodontosaurids)
            └── Coelurosauria
                ├── Tyrannosauroidea
                ├── Ornithomimosauria
                └── Maniraptora
                    ├── Therizinosauridae
                    ├── Oviraptorosauria
                    └── Paraves
                        ├── Dromaeosauridae
                        └── Avialae (Birds)
```

## How New Discoveries Change the Tree

Dinosaur classification is constantly being refined. Recent examples:

- **Baron et al. (2017)** proposed a radical reshuffling that would group theropods with ornithischians rather than sauropodomorphs. While controversial, it demonstrates that even fundamental classifications remain debatable.
- New fossils from China and Argentina regularly add branches or cause rearrangements in the family tree.
- Statistical methods like Bayesian analysis are providing more nuanced pictures of evolutionary relationships.

## Naming Conventions

Dinosaur names follow **binomial nomenclature**: a genus name and a species name, always italicized:
- *Tyrannosaurus* (genus) *rex* (species)
- The genus is capitalized, the species is not
- After first use, the genus can be abbreviated: *T. rex*

Names often reflect physical features, locations, or people:
- *Triceratops* = "three-horned face"
- *Albertosaurus* = "Alberta lizard"
- *Lambeosaurus* = "Lambe's lizard" (named after paleontologist Lawrence Lambe)

> **Key Takeaway:** Dinosaurs are classified into two major orders (Saurischia and Ornithischia) based on hip structure, but modern cladistic analysis reveals a much more complex family tree. Classification is an active area of research, with new discoveries regularly refining our understanding of dinosaur relationships.
